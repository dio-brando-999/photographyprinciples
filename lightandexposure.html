Light & Exposure

Photography is capturing the light of a scene and freezing the moment. As a result, it is important to understand the four key factors that photographers can control to determine a photograph’s end result. All photographs ever taken have three elements when it comes to the exposure of the image: Aperture, Shutter Speed & ISO

Aperture- the physical AMOUNT of light being let in by a camera’s lens (ex. f/8) Shutter Speed- the amount of TIME the photograph is exposed for (ex. 1/1000th) ISO (as referred to as ISO speed)- the camera’s “SENSITIVITY” to light (ISO 100)

Aperture: corresponds to how “open” the lens is. The numerical value is called an f-stop. The smaller the f-stop value (ex. f/1.4, f/2.8), the more light is being let in (and therefore the wider the lens is “open”). These photography terms such as a “wide-open” or a “fast” lens refer to the widest aperture or how much the lens can be open respectively. However, the faster the aperture, the less depth of field the photo has. Aperture control the depth of field, or the amount of the image that will be in focus. A shallow depth of field is great for isolating subjects from their backgrounds, such as a portrait, while a large depth of field is great for including both the subject and the background, such as documentary or photojournalism.

Shutter Speed: how fast the camera’s shutter open and closes to take a picture. Some photos can be exposed at the shutter speed of 1/4000th of a second (freezing the action of a scene extremely well, but only letting in a small amount of light) or 1/3rd of a second (blurring any motion, while letting in more light compared to a 1/4000th exposure) A good rule to follow to reduce motion blur is to take your lens focal length and set the shutter speed to be 1/focal length.
