Story/Narrative

Whether through a single photo or a endless photo projects, photos have the power to tell stories. So, here are some things to keep in mind when photographing for a story

Blueprint: How to tell a story

One of my favorite ways of taking photos is to tell the stories of the people around me. Whether that be photos of family get-togethers or my friends from high school, photos have the ability to convey a narrative through what are known as “decisive moments”, noted by french photographer Henri Cartier-Bresson.

So naturally, the next question is: how do we accomplish this? Each story is unique and therefore will need different ways to approach or execute. However, you can generally follow some critical steps that will help make your story connect with viewers.

1. Brainstorming a story

If you are having trouble coming up with a story, start with something simple and small. The story doesn’t have to be something mind-blowing. To start, think of something that applies to what you enjoy or yourself. Tell the story of a day in your life, your neighbor and where you live, landscapes of where you live, or local events. The best thing to do is to shoot.

2. Think about your shots

The next best thing you can do is think about what you are shooting and how you will capture the essence of the “story” through your photos. You can do this by creating a shot-list. Typically, there are two common categories of shots you encounter when telling a story. Context These are the types of shots that set the setting of your story. They answer the “Where?”, “When?”, “What?” and sometimes “How?” questions. You can do this through wider-angled shots that show the location of an event, the people involved, etc. Decisive Moments These are the shots that happen at the moment and that are generally spontaneous and unplanned. These can be hard to account for while creating a shot-list, but it can extremely helpful to spend some time beforehand thinking about what your decisive moment may be in your time before actually shooting.

3. Shooting

Now that you have everything ready like your shot-list, camera, and lenses, it is time to shoot! Go out in the field and have fun by incoperating all of these things!
